How to understand critical reading

Critical reading：It is a kind of activate reading with a reading purpose in which readers may ask questions, make inferences and evaluate this ideas, etc*

| Missions/Readers| A non-critical reader | A critical reader |
| ———————————————————— | ———————————————————— |
|Reading Purpose | What the text says| 1What the text does/describes
2what the text means|
|**Focus*** | Learn the facts of the situation or discover an accepted interpretation of those events | appreciate how a particular perspective on the events and a particular selection of facts can lead to particular understanding* |

Target:
1* gaining deeper meaning of the text

2* understanding how the meaning is conveyed

How to read critically

How to Read Critically 1: Asking Questions and Making Judgement

  • Method 1: Asking questions

    1* making predictions
    2* understanding the author’s tone or purpose
    3* clarify doubts
    4* recognize bias
    4* Improve your comprehension by getting truly involved in reading

Result: Help you improve your comprehension by getting truly involved in reading

Examples of critcal comprehension questons:

Why did the author write this article or book?
What is the big idea or big picture of it?
How well-developed are the themes?
What is the author trying to say?
What is the main argument being presented?
What alternatives are there?
How appropriate are the comparisons that are used?
Could this possibly happen?
***

  • Method 2: Exercise your judgement when reading

    whether the conclusions are supported firmly by the argument or not?
    whether you are convinced by the author?

How to Read Critically 2: Making Inferences

  • Method 3: Making inferences
    To reach a deeper understanding of the implied meanings

Inference :an essential skill in reading comprehension, refers to drawing logical and rational * implied and indirectly stated conclusions

Make inferecnes and draw a conclusion based on

  • use hints or clues between the lines
  • textual evidence in a text to support your inferences
  • the context clues given in the text like facts or circumstance
  • general knowledge or commonn sense
  • own worldview and personal experiences

Scientific Essay

  • Characteristics of Scientific Writing
    1* formal register of English
    2* high degree of nominalization
    3* conciseness
    4* completeness
    5* objectivity

  • Structure of Scientific Essay
    1* Abstract

    * Background
* Researuch Purpose
* Material and Method
* Results
* Conclusion
* Values of significance

    2* Introduction

    * Background
* Literature Review
* Research Gap
* Research Purpose

    3* Material and method

    * Material: subjects and samples
* Method: Theretical or Empirical / Qualitative or Quantitative
* Experiment Setup /Research Setup
* Procedure (should be described)

    4* Results

    * Use one sentence to describe the table or figure*
* Briefly introduce the most importance results
* Give comments on the results and generalize the deduction from the results

    5* Discussion

    * Analyze the data
* Point out the limitations and doubts
* Express viewpoints and how the results and interpretations agree/contrast with the previous published work
* State the theoretical implicationns and practical application of the study*
* Arrive at a conclusion
*

    6* Conclusion

    • Echo the introduction
    • Summarize the main findings of the research
    • Emphasize the importance of the paper or redirect the readers’ thought process

    7* References

    • Show respect to the previous works
    • Facilitate the literature search
    • Name and Year System
    • Citation Order System

###Note: Week3

Features of Academic English

  • it uses formal academic language and avoids colloquialisms (formal in tone and impersonal in style)
  • it avoids personal pronouns such as l, me, you, your, and is written in the third person
  • it is objective and impartial (free from undue bias or preconceived opinions)
  • it often uses impersonal structures (the passive voice)
  • it may use specialized vocabulary
  • it avoids shortened forms of verbs, such as won’t, doesn’t or it’s but in complete form
  • it avoids verbs that are composed of multiple words, such as ‘give up’, ‘put up with’ but individual ones*
  • the texts are well-structured
  • there are well-developed paragraphs which often start with a strong topic sentence
  • there are linking words which give the text cohesion
  • there are lots of references to other writers
  • it is tentative and cautious (tends to employ a cautious way of explaining findings, using expressions such as ‘may’, ‘it is possible that***’, ‘could’)

###Note: Week5

Effective reading
Stage:

Stage 1

  • Raise questions in advance and think of keywords you are looking
    for
  • What do you hope/expect to find?
  • Note down these questions & keywords
  • Anticipation means you read with more focus
  • This is the first step to active reading

Stage 2

  1. 1st reading: Skim the text for general meaning
  • Only take very brief notes at this stage, if any, then use a pencil to mark the passages you need
  1. 2nd reading: Scan the text
    • for the keywords/ particular topics you noted at Stage 1 + your marked passages
  2. 3rd reading: Read for detail.
  • Now go back over the text carefully. Read each paragraph/section in detail. Take selective notes, capturing some detail. Because you have carried out a skim and a scan read already, the read for detail stage is more effective

Effective reading for science
Generally, scientific papers tend to follow a standardized structure. There may be some variation within this for some sections, but the core sections are usually as follows:

Structure:

  • Title
  • Author details
  • Abstract
  • Introduction
  • Theoretical discussion
  • Presentation and discussion of empirical findings
  • Conclusion
  • Bibliography

Tips for reading abstract 摘要的阅读

  1. Isolate each statement in the abstract
    Perhaps use colour/highlighter to identify them

  2. Match these against the whole paper, identifying which section of the text relates to each part of the abstract

  3. Colour-code paragraphs to show which part of the abstract they relate to

###Note: Week6

Academic Reading

Skimming for information:

1) the title and subtitles;
2) the abstract (if there is one);
3) the introduction and thesis;
4) the first or last paragraphs of the essays / chapters;
5) headings and subheadings;
6) the conclusion and summaries;
7) the first or the last sentence of each paragraph;
8) the highlighted text such as words in bold or italic script;
9) the titles of graphs, tables or diagrams.

Scanning for information:

1) what you are searching for
(e.g,, a definition of the term, an effect of the phenomenon, the
objective or the methods of the paper);
2) search thesis statements and topic sentences in paragraphs;
3) use the knowledge of essay organization (such as cause-effect,
comparison-contrast, problem-solution) to find what you are looking
for;
4) pay attention to signal words such as numbers, steps, words like first,
second, next, or words that are bold- -faced, in italics, or in different
sizes, styles.

Introductory section

1) Introduce your topic and provide the relevant background information /
context of the topic field (including the importance of the research).
2) Give clear definitions of the key terms.
3) Review the previous related studies and point out their limitations if you can.
4) State the purpose / objective of your study and set out the research questions
to he addressed.
5) Introduce the theoretical perspectives or / and the method to be used.
6) Explain how the paper will be organized the outline of the paper.

Week13 —-summary Tests

How to Write a Summary

  1. What is a summary?
    • A summary is a brief explanation or account of a piece of writing.
    • You will need to include only the main idea and supporting facts.
      幻灯片4
  2. The purpose of a summary
  • To give the reader a clear, objective picture of the original lecture or text.
  • Most importantly, the summary restates the main points of a text or a lecture without giving details, such as dates, numbers or statistics.

  1. Steps of writing a summary

    • Skim the piece.
    • Read the piece thoroughly.
    • Outline the article.
    • write:

    1. Start with a clear identification of the work.
      Clearly identify (in the present tense) the background information needed for your summary:
      the type of work, title, author, and main point.
      e.g.: In the featured article “Five Kinds of Learning,” the author, Holland Oates, justifies his opinion on the hot topic of learning styles and adds a few himself.

    2. Summarize the piece as a whole.
      Omit nothing important and strive for overall coherence through appropriate transitions. Write using “summarizing language.” Your reader needs to be reminded that this is not your own work. Use phrases like the article claims, the author suggests, etc.
      Present the material in a neutral fashion. Your opinions, ideas, and interpretations should be left in your brain – don’t put them into your summary. Be conscious of choosing your words. Only include what was in the original work.
      Be concise. This is a summary – it should be much shorter than the original piece. If you’re working on an article, give yourself a target length of 1/4 the original article.

    3. Conclude with a final statement.
      This is not a statement of your own point of view, however; it should reflect the significance of the book or article from the author’s standpoint.

4. Check for accuracy.
A summary is slightly different than any other creative work -- you must maintain a voice that's current with the author's 100% of the time. Revisit the article as you go over your work -- are you jumping to any conclusions? Have you omitted anything important? Have you concentrated too much on the finer details?

Skills practiced

  1. note-taking
  2. paraphrasing (using your own words and sentence structure)
  3. condensing examples

Revision

  1. Start your summary with a clear identification of the type of work, title, author, and main point in the present tense.
    Example: In the feature article “Four Kinds of Reading,” the author, Donald Hall, explains his opinion about different types of reading.

  2. Check with your outline and your original to make sure you have covered the important points.

  3. Never put any of your own ideas, opinions, or interpretations into the summary. This means you have to be very careful of your word choice.

  4. Write using “summarizing language.” Periodically remind your reader that this is a summary by using phrases such as the article claims, the author suggests, etc.

  5. Write a complete bibliographic citation at the beginning of your summary. A complete bibliographic citation includes as a minimum, the title of the work, the author, the source.
